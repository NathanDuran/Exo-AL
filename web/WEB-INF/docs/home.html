Exo-AL

Finding Exo-planets with Active Learning AI

Welcome to the Exo-AL project! Our goal is to try and find exoplanet candidates in data
collected by NASA's Kepler spacecraft. To accomplish this we are using an Artificial
Intelligence system that is capable of classifying stars as either exoplanet hosts or non-
hosts. When the AI systems 'confidence' is low for a stars classification it will ask for
human assistance in a process called Active Learning. That's where you come in. On the
classify page of this site you will be shown a series of stars 'light curves' and asked
whether you think it may, or may not, be host to an orbiting exoplanet. By asking you to
help the AI to classify stars we hope to improve its ability to classify stars in the future.
Think of it as 'teaching' the AI to be better at its job.

Don't worry if you don't know what an exoplanet or light curve is! You can find out
more about exoplanets and how we find them, as well as active learning below.

The Instructions page will provide you with all the information you need to start
classifying.

The Results page will show you the initial accuracy of the classifier (without any human
help) and the current accuracy (with human help).

What are Exo-planets and how do we find them?

An exoplanet, or extrasolar planet, is a planet which orbits a star outside of the solar
system. There are many compelling reasons to find and study exoplanets that result in a
more detailed knowledge of our universe. The location, size and composition of
exoplanets help refine our understanding of the development of our own solar system
as well as others. A perhaps more exciting reason, is the possibility of discovering planet
similar to earth, or perhaps 7 as was the case for Trappist-'s astonishing recent discovery.

Finding 'earth like worlds' was a primary goal of the Kepler mission, the most successful
exoplanet discovery project to date, with ~2300 of the ~3400 confirmed planets
attributed to the Kepler mission. Launched in March 2009 the spacecraft spend over
four years monitoring more than 160,000 stars in a fixed region of the sky known as
Cygnus-Lyra. By observing the same set of stars for long periods of time Kepler was able
to accumulate data for the brightness of each star over time, also known as a 'light curve'.

There are several techniques for discovering exoplanets but the most successful (and
the one used by the Kepler team and this project) is the Transit Method. Detection using
the transit method is analogous to looking for an eclipse or an event such as the transit
of Venus. As a planet passes in front of the host star the light of the star is dimmed
because the light is blocked by the planet. When a star is observed over a period of time,
if a number of these 'transit events' occur at regular periods then the existence of a
planet can be inferred. Typically the dip in intensity is around 1% or much less, for
example observing a planet the size of earth around a similar star would result in a drop
of only 0.01%.

What is Active Learning?

The traditional machine learning approach has already been shown to be highly
successful when applied to finding exoplanets, though it has never achieved 100%
accuracy and lacks the ability to adapt to new and possibly unusual instances of data.
The active learning approach aims to combine two paradigms with the hope that
their individual strengths and weakness complement each other when used together.
The relatively slow classification ability of humans can be mitigated by the
speed of a machine learner, and the 'inflexible' machine learning algorithm can be
improved with the more 'sophisticated' human classification ability.

This project takes your feedback (classifications) and uses it to update the AI machine
learner and hopefully improve its classification ability when applied to future unseen
data. The number of instances required to be shown to the user is kept to a minimum
because only those uncertain or incorrect instances must be labelled.

First the AI machine learner is trained using a set of labelled data where we know the
stars are either exoplanet hosts or not. Then, the same AI is applied to a set of data
where we do not know the labels and produces a 'confidence' value for its chosen label.
For every star that it has less than 70% confidence in its chosen label it will ask users to
make a decision and label the star as either exoplanet host or not. These decisions will
then be used to re-train the AI and hopefully improve its ability to classify stars in the
future.
