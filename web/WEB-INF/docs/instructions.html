Instructions

Controls

On the classify page you will be shown stars light curves, which are graphs of a stars
brightness over time. The goal is to look for the tell-tale 'dips' in luminosity that occur
when a planet passes in front of its host star, also known as a transit. It is important to
make the distinction between 'gaps' and 'dips'. Any gaps you see in the light curve will be
caused by other factors not related to an exoplanet. We are looking for areas where the
luminosity has dipped lower but has still been recorded. There are some light curves,
with their transits highlighted, in the examples section.

PLEASE NOTE: The light curve display works best in Chrome or Safari Browsers.


At the top of the page the current star ID is displayed along with the quarter number
being viewed.

Using the slider at the bottom of the light curve you can give each star a score
depending on how confident you are that it has one or more transit-like features.

1 to 100 (green), where 1 is not confident at all and 100 is positive, indicates that you
think it has transit-like features and therefore may have an orbiting exoplanet.

-1 to -100 (red) indicates that you think it does not have transit like features and
therefore may not have an orbiting exoplanet.

0 indicates that you are not sure either way.

The submit button records your chosen score for the star and loads the next star for you
to view.

The next and back buttons on either side of the light curve let you cycle through different
light curves for the current star. These were generated over different yearly quarters of
the Kepler mission.

The light curve itself can be zoomed and panned using the left mouse button and scroll
wheel, though performance may vary on browsers other than Chrome and Safari.

It is perfectly ok to be unsure about a particular light curve. For many of the light curves
you view it will be very difficult to distinguish any transit-like features if there are any.
Generally it is more helpful to provide some score rather than 0, even if it is very small.

Using the next and back buttons on either side of the light curve is often helpful if you are
unsure. Light curves generated over different periods of time can reveal features or
simply be clearer than others. It is also important to note that a light curve for one
quarter may not contain a transit while another does, simply because planets take time
to orbit their star. If we were viewing our sun for example, we would have to view at
least four quarters worth of light curves to spot earths transit signal.

Examples

The following are some examples of light curves that contain planetary transitions with
some or all of the transitions highlighted with red boxes. It is also an opportunity to
see some of the variety of light curves you may encounter.


Light curve for KIC: 757450 3rd Quarter. An example of very distinct transitions.
Four of the eight or so transitions have been highlighted.


Light curve for KIC: 4049131 1st Quarter. An example of shallow transitions.
One transition has been highlighted, with another visible on the bottom right.


Light curve for KIC: 4056616 4th Quarter. An example of very shallow transitions.
Four of the transitions have been highlighted. This is an example of a light curve that
the AI is likely to classify with low confidence.


Light curve for KIC: 11018648 2nd Quarter. An example of a 'noisy' star but with very
distinct transitions.


Light curve for KIC: 3102384 2nd Quarter. An example of an erratic light curve with distinct
transitions and lots of other false transition-like signals.


Light curve for KIC: 6032497 1st Quarter. An example of an extremely noisy light curve.
The transitions are nearly impossible to distinguish from the natural fluctuations of the star.
